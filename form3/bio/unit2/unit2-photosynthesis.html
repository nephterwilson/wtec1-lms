Overview of Photosynthesis

üåø

Photosynthesis is the process by which green plants make their own food using sunlight, carbon dioxide, and water.

Carbon dioxide + Water ‚Üí (Light energy, Chlorophyll) ‚Üí Glucose + Oxygen

Importance of Photosynthesis

  • Produces oxygen necessary for respiration.
  • Provides food for plants and other organisms.
  • Maintains the carbon dioxide-oxygen balance in the atmosphere.

Parts and Functions of a Leaf

üçÉ

The leaf has several parts, each performing specific functions essential for photosynthesis and plant survival.

Parts of a Leaf

  • Lamina: Flat surface for light absorption.
  • Petiole: Attaches leaf to stem.
  • Veins: Transport water (xylem) and food (phloem).
  • Stomata: Pores for gas exchange.

Functions of a Leaf

  • Photosynthesis ‚Äì production of food.
  • Transpiration ‚Äì water loss for cooling.
  • Gas exchange ‚Äì oxygen and carbon dioxide diffusion.

Internal Structure of a Leaf

  • Cuticle: Waxy layer that reduces water loss.
  • Upper Epidermis: Transparent for light penetration.
  • Palisade Mesophyll: Packed with chloroplasts for photosynthesis.
  • Spongy Mesophyll: Has air spaces for gas exchange.
  • Lower Epidermis: Contains stomata controlled by guard cells.

Parts and Functions of a Plant Cell

üß´

Plant cells contain specialized organelles that support photosynthesis and other life processes.

  • Cell Wall: Provides support and structure.
  • Cell Membrane: Controls movement of substances.
  • Nucleus: Controls activities, contains DNA.
  • Cytoplasm: Site of chemical reactions.
  • Chloroplasts: Contain chlorophyll for photosynthesis.
  • Mitochondria: Produce energy through respiration.
  • Vacuole: Stores water, maintains turgidity.
  • Endoplasmic Reticulum: Synthesizes and transports materials.
  • Golgi Apparatus: Packages and modifies proteins and lipids.
  • Ribosomes: Sites of protein synthesis.
  • Plasmodesmata: Channels for material exchange between cells.

Adaptations of Leaves for Photosynthesis

üåø

Leaves have specialized structures to maximize photosynthesis efficiency.

  • It has chlorophyll that traps sunlight energy for photosynthesis.
  • It has a broad surface area that captures more sunlight.
  • It has a thin blade that allows easy diffusion of gases.
  • It has veins that transport water and minerals and carry food away.
  • It has air spaces that enhance gas exchange within the leaf.
  • It has a transparent cuticle that allows light to pass through.
  • It has stomata that regulate gas exchange and control water loss.

Stages of Photosynthesis

‚ö°

Photosynthesis occurs in two main stages: the light-dependent and light-independent (Calvin Cycle) stages.

Light-Dependent Stage

  • Occurs in the grana of chloroplasts.
  • Light energy splits water into oxygen, hydrogen ions, and electrons (photolysis).
  • Produces ATP and NADPH for the next stage.

Light-Independent Stage (Calvin Cycle)

  • Occurs in the chloroplast stroma.
  • Uses ATP and NADPH to convert CO‚ÇÇ and hydrogen into glucose.

Fate of Glucose After Photosynthesis

  • Used in respiration for energy.
  • Stored as starch in roots, leaves, and tubers.
  • Converted into sucrose for transport.
  • Used to form cellulose for cell walls.
  • Converted into lipids for storage in seeds.
  • Combined with nitrates to form proteins.

Fate of Glucose After Photosynthesis

  • Used in respiration for energy ‚Äî glucose is broken down in cells to release energy required for growth, repair, and other life processes.
  • Stored as starch in roots, leaves, and tubers ‚Äî excess glucose is converted into starch, an insoluble form, for future use when photosynthesis is not taking place.
  • Converted into sucrose for transport ‚Äî sucrose, a soluble sugar, is transported through the phloem to other parts of the plant where it is needed.
  • Used to form cellulose for cell walls ‚Äî glucose molecules are linked together to produce cellulose, which strengthens and supports plant cell walls.
  • Converted into lipids for storage in seeds ‚Äî glucose can be changed into fats and oils that provide energy reserves for germinating seeds.
  • Combined with nitrates to form proteins ‚Äî glucose joins with nitrogen compounds from the soil to produce amino acids, which build plant proteins.

Mineral Elements in Photosynthesis

üß≤

Different minerals are essential for photosynthesis and plant health.

  • Carbon (C): Used to form glucose.
  • Potassium (K): Controls stomata and enzyme activity.
  • Nitrogen (N): Component of chlorophyll and proteins and amino acids.
  • Phosphorus (P): Key part of ATP and aids cell division..
  • Oxygen (O): Released as a by-product.
  • Sulphur (S): Supports enzyme function.
  • Hydrogen (H): Combines with CO‚ÇÇ to make glucose.
  • Magnesium (Mg): Central atom in chlorophyll.
  • Iron (Fe): Needed for chlorophyll formation and electron transport.

Types of Pigments in Leaves

  • Chlorophyll: Green pigment absorbing blue and red light.
  • Carotene: Yellow-orange pigment assisting photosynthesis.
  • Xanthophyll: Yellow pigment helping in light absorption.

Activity: Investigating Types of Pigments in Plant Leaves

This activity demonstrates how to separate different pigments found in green leaves using the process of chromatography.

Requirements:

  • Fresh green leaves
  • Alcohol (ethanol)
  • Filter paper
  • Tape
  • Beakers (small and large)
  • Mortar and pestle
  • Glass rod

Procedure:

  • Collect fresh green leaves and crush them into small pieces using a mortar and pestle.
  • Place the crushed leaves in a small beaker and add alcohol until it is half full.
  • Place the beaker in a larger water bath and heat gently until the alcohol turns green as pigments dissolve.
  • Remove the beaker and filter the green pigment solution into another clean beaker.
  • Cut a strip of filter paper (about 3 cm √ó 10 cm) and attach it to a glass rod with tape.
  • Hang the paper strip so that its tip just touches the surface of the pigment solution.
  • Leave it undisturbed for about 30 minutes to allow the pigments to separate along the paper.
  • Observe the different colored bands formed on the filter paper, each representing a different pigment.
