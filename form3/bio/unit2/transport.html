Transport in Plants

Understanding how water, minerals, and food move through plants to support growth and life.

Introduction to Transport in Plants

Transport in plants refers to the movement of substances from one part of the plant to another to support vital processes such as photosynthesis, growth, and respiration.

  • Plants make their own food through photosynthesis using carbon dioxide and water.
  • Water and minerals are absorbed from the soil through the roots.
  • Glucose produced in the leaves must be transported to other plant parts for energy and growth.
  • Efficient transport systems are essential because plants are multicellular with organs far apart.

Need for a Transport System in Plants

A transport system in plants is vital for movement of substances to sustain life functions:

  • Water and mineral salts from the roots must reach the leaves for photosynthesis.
  • Manufactured food from the leaves must move to roots, stems, and growing regions.
  • Transport maintains water balance and nutrient distribution across the plant.
  • Without transport tissues, plants would die due to lack of supply to distant cells.

Vascular Bundles: The Transport Tissues

Vascular bundles are specialized conducting tissues responsible for transporting substances within plants. They consist of two main types:

  • Xylem: Transports water and mineral salts from roots to leaves.
  • Phloem: Transports manufactured food from leaves to other parts of the plant.

Vascular bundles run continuously through roots, stems, and leaves, ensuring efficient movement of substances throughout the plant.

Xylem Tissue

Xylem is a tissue made mostly of dead cells that form tubes to transport water and minerals upward from roots to leaves.

Main Features:

  • Made of long, hollow, dead cells joined end-to-end forming continuous tubes.
  • Contains two cell types: tracheids (in all plants) and vessel elements (mainly in flowering plants).
  • Walls have lignin for strength and support.
  • Walls contain pits allowing water to move sideways between cells.
  • Cells lack cytoplasm and nuclei, allowing free water flow.
  • Provides mechanical support to keep the plant upright.

Characteristics of Xylem Tubes

  • Composed of dead cells with no cytoplasm or nuclei.
  • Walls made of cellulose and lignin.
  • Contain pits for lateral water movement.
  • Give mechanical strength and rigidity to the plant.

Phloem Tissue

Phloem is made of living cells that transport food substances, mainly sugars like sucrose, from leaves to other parts of the plant.

Main Features:

  • Consists of sieve tube elements and companion cells.
  • Sieve tube elements have perforated cross walls called sieve plates.
  • Companion cells contain nuclei and organelles to maintain sieve tubes.
  • Phloem walls are not lignified and cells contain cytoplasm.
  • Translocation of food is bi-directional (up or down).

Characteristics of Phloem Tubes

  • Made of living sieve elements.
  • Contain cytoplasm but no lignin in walls.
  • Sieve plates at ends of sieve tubes allow food movement.
  • Each sieve element has a companion cell for control and energy supply.

Summary of Differences Between Xylem and Phloem

Structural Differences:

  • Xylem cells are dead; phloem cells are living.
  • Xylem cells lack nuclei; phloem companion cells contain nuclei.
  • Xylem walls are lignified; phloem walls are not.
  • Xylem has no cross walls; phloem has sieve plates with pores.

Functional Differences:

  • Xylem transports water and minerals upward from roots to leaves.
  • Phloem transports manufactured food from leaves to all plant parts.
  • Xylem provides mechanical support; phloem does not.
