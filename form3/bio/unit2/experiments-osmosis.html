Investigations on Osmosis

Understanding How Water Moves Through Living Cells

Investigation 1: Osmosis in Living Tissue (Potatoes)

This investigation demonstrates osmosis using fresh potatoes and sugar to observe water movement through living cells.

Aim

To investigate osmosis in living tissue using fresh potatoes.

Materials

  • Fresh potatoes (Irish or sweet)
  • Water
  • Sugar
  • Large beakers or dish
  • Scalpel

Procedure

  • Cut two fresh potatoes so they can stand upright.
  • Peel the sides near the base and scoop cavities at the top of each potato.
  • Label them A and B, place in a dish with a little water.
  • Pour water into potato A and sugar into potato B.
  • Leave undisturbed for 24 hours.

Results

  • Potato A: No visible water movement.
  • Potato B: Water moved into sugar cavity via osmosis.

Conclusion

Osmosis causes water to move from a dilute region to a concentrated one. In potato B, sugar created lower water potential, pulling water into the cavity â€” demonstrating osmosis in living tissue.

Investigation 2: Effects of Osmosis on Living Cells

This experiment shows how different salt concentrations affect potato strips, revealing the visible effects of osmosis.

Aim

To find out the effect of osmosis on living plant cells.

Materials

  • 2 potato strips (2cm Ã— 2cm Ã— 4cm)
  • 2 beakers
  • Scalpel
  • Distilled water
  • 50% salt solution

Procedure

  • Peel and cut two equal potato strips.
  • In beaker A, add 50% salt solution; in beaker B, add distilled water.
  • Place one strip in each and leave for 20 minutes.
  • Observe and compare length and texture of the strips.

Results

  • Potato A: Became soft and shorter (flaccid).
  • Potato B: Became firm and longer (turgid).

Discussion

In beaker A, water left the cells into the salt solution, causing plasmolysis. In beaker B, water entered cells, creating turgor pressure that supports non-woody plants by maintaining structure and rigidity.
