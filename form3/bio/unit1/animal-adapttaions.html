Adaptations of Plants and Animals to Various Environments

How plants and animals adjust to survive in different habitats

Mesophytes

üåø

Mesophytes are plants that grow under average water and temperature conditions, forming grasslands and forests.

Adaptations

  • Thin leaves for rapid diffusion of gases during photosynthesis.
  • Broad, flat leaf blades for more light and carbon dioxide absorption.
  • Mosaic leaf arrangement ensures maximum sunlight exposure.
  • Stomata on both leaf surfaces for efficient gaseous exchange.
  • Spongy mesophyll layer allows free circulation of gases.
  • Leaves contain chlorophyll for capturing sunlight energy.
  • Thick transparent cuticle reduces water loss.
  • Well-developed root system for water absorption.

Xerophytes

üåµ

Xerophytes are plants that grow in dry environments like deserts. They have special features for conserving water.

Adaptations

  • Extensive root systems for absorbing deep or distant water.
  • Water storage tissues in stems (succulents like cacti and aloe).
  • Thick waxy cuticle and hairy leaves to reduce evaporation.
  • Small leaves and thorny stems minimize surface area and protect from animals.
  • Few stomata to reduce water loss through transpiration.
  • Seeds remain dormant during dry seasons and germinate after rains.

Hydrophytes

üíß

Hydrophytes are plants that live in or near water, such as lilies and reeds. They are adapted to moist or aquatic environments.

Adaptations

  • Very thin or no cuticle to absorb water and gases directly.
  • Roots mainly anchor plants; absorption occurs through body surfaces.
  • Air spaces in stems and leaves aid buoyancy and gas exchange.
  • Have little xylem; support comes from aerenchyma tissue.
  • Submerged leaves lack stomata, while floating ones have many on upper surfaces.
