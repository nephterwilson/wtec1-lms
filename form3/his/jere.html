The Epic Journey Under Zwangendaba

The Jere Ngoni Under Zwangendaba

Origin & Leadership

Origin: Northern Zululand

Leader: Zwangendaba, son of Hlatshayo, who had served in Zwide's army. Zwangendaba himself was a commander under Zwide.

Composition

The group included Swazi, Tsonga, Nsenga, and Karanga people who were later assimilated into the Ngoni nation.

Reasons for Migration

  1. Instability caused by Shaka's wars (Mfecane period)
  2. Desire to establish their own independent kingdom
  3. Shortage of land and resources in their homeland
  4. Search for cattle and safety from conflict
  5. Escape from powerful rulers in South Africa

The Migration Journey

1819 - Departure
Left Zululand after Zwide's defeat by Shaka's forces
Early Journey
Passed through Swaziland, attacking Swazi and Tsonga tribes along the way
Mozambique Entry
Entered Mozambique after crossing the Limpopo River
1831 - Delagoa Bay
Lived briefly with Soshangane and Nxaba at Delagoa Bay but fought and were defeated
Zimbabwe Invasion
Moved west into Zimbabwe, destroying Rozwi centres at Khami and Dhlodhlo
19 November 1835 - Zambezi Crossing
Crossed the Zambezi River near Zumbo (the day M'mbelwa was born - a significant date in Ngoni history)
Zambia Entry
Attacked the Nsenga of Petauke in Zambia
19 September 1840 - Malawi Arrival
Entered Malawi at Mabiri, fought the Tumbuka and Chewa peoples
1844 - Tanzania Movement
Later moved to Ufipa in Tanzania
1845 - Final Settlement
Settled finally at Mapupo

The Succession Crisis After Zwangendaba's Death (1848)

What is a Crisis?

A crisis is a serious conflict or struggle for power that threatens stability and can lead to division or violence.

What Happened

  • Zwangendaba died at Mapupo in 1848
  • He had chosen M'mbelwa, the son of a younger wife, as his successor
  • Some elders opposed him and supported Mpezeni, the eldest son
  • Ntabeni, Zwangendaba's brother, became regent (temporary ruler)
  • When Ntabeni declared Mpezeni as heir, supporters of M'mbelwa rebelled
  • This led to the division of the Ngoni into separate groups

Mzimba: The Heart of M'mbelwa's Kingdom

Why Mzimba Was Chosen

  • Rich grazing land free from tsetse flies
  • Enough water sources and fertile land for agriculture
  • Space for settlement and farming activities
  • Strategic location for defense and control

Mzimba Under the British

  • Missionaries advised Harry Johnston not to attack the Ngoni
  • Peaceful negotiations followed between British and Ngoni leaders
  • Mzimba became part of the British Protectorate on 24 October 1904
  • Hector Macdonald was appointed as the first British resident
  • Ngoni agreed to stop raids and pay taxes to the colonial government
