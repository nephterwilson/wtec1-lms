The Ndebele Kingdom

The Matabele - Men of the Long Shield

1. Origin and Early Life of Mzilikazi

Founder: Mzilikazi Khumalo, son of Moshobane

Original Position: Leader of the Khumalo clan in Zulu territory (South Africa)

Early History

  • After the death of Dingiswayo (1817), Shaka incorporated the Khumalo clan into the Zulu nation
  • Mzilikazi became a Zulu general under King Shaka
  • He was known for his military prowess and leadership abilities

2. Cause of Migration (1821)

The Great Rebellion

  • In 1821, Mzilikazi was sent by Shaka to raid the Sotho people
  • After a successful raid, he kept the captured cattle and loot instead of returning them to Shaka
  • Fearing Shaka's severe punishment for this act of defiance, Mzilikazi fled Zululand
  • He left with about 300 followers into the Drakensberg Mountains

3. The Migration Route of the Ndebele

Mzilikazi's journey can be divided into four major phases:

(a) Ekhupumuleni (1824) ‚Äì "The Resting Place"

First Settlement in the Transvaal Region

  • Raided Tswana and Sotho communities for cattle, women, and children
  • Their long shields earned them the name "Ndebele" (Matabele) ‚Äì meaning "men of the long shield"

Reasons for Leaving Ekhupumuleni:

  • Attacks from Pedi warriors
  • Fear of Shaka's revenge
  • Drought and poor grazing land
(b) Emhlahlandlela (1826) ‚Äì Central Transvaal

New Capital and Missionary Contact

  • Established new capital; raided the Sotho and Tswana again
  • Robert Moffat, a missionary, visited in 1829 and befriended Mzilikazi
  • However, this area was too close to Zululand, attracting attacks from Dingane (Shaka's brother)
  • The Ndebele also fought Kora, Khoisan, Griqua, Pedi, and Rolong groups

Result: Mzilikazi abandoned the area due to constant conflicts

(c) Egabeni and Mosega Settlements

Along the Marico River

  • Egabeni (along Marico River): drove away the Hurutshe people and continued raids
  • Mzilikazi met Robert Moffat and Dr Andrew Smith again
  • Mosega: later became a more permanent settlement

Reasons for Leaving Mosega:

  • Boer attacks led by Hendrik Potgieter after the Ndebele raided their cattle
  • Dingane's Zulu raids from the south
  • Poor pastures and grazing land
  • United resistance by Kora and Griqua tribes
(d) Crossing into Zimbabwe (c.1838‚Äì1840)

Final Migration North

Mzilikazi decided to move northwards across the Limpopo River into present-day Zimbabwe.

The Group Split into Two:

Group 1 (Mzilikazi): Mainly warriors, went through Botswana, raiding as they moved

Group 2 (Gundwane Mkulumane): Included women, children, and elderly, moved directly into western Zimbabwe and founded Nthaba-ye-Zinduna (Ntaba-zinduna)

Crisis - The Succession Dispute:

  • Gundwane and others thought Mzilikazi was dead and crowned Mkulumane as king
  • When Mzilikazi arrived later, he saw this as treason
  • He executed Gundwane and his supporters
  • Mkulumane's fate is unknown ‚Äì he disappeared from history
  • Mzilikazi then established his final capital at Inyati (near Bulawayo)

4. Organisation of the Ndebele Kingdom

(a) The Monarchy

Centralised Government

The kingdom was ruled under a strong centralised system with the King (Inkosi) at the top

King's Powers

Controlled judicial, political, military, and economic affairs of the nation

Wealth Control

All cattle and resources belonged to the king, who redistributed them to his subjects

Advisory Council

The king ruled with help from indunas (chiefs) and advisors

(b) The Army

The army was the pillar of the kingdom and central to Ndebele power

  • Young men trained as soldiers (impi); could not marry until they had fought bravely
  • Operated under strict discipline and lived separately from civilians
  • Received special privileges (extra meat and milk)
  • The army maintained order, collected tribute, and defended the state

5. Division of Ndebele Society

Class Description
AbeZansi (Zansi) Original Khumalo and their close followers from Zululand. Formed the ruling elite and army generals.
AbeNhla (Enhla) Assimilated peoples such as the Sotho, Tswana, Kora, and Griqua who joined during migration.
Amaholi (Maholi) Indigenous people of Zimbabwe (Shona, Rozwi, Karanga, Tjabi). Initially treated as servants or serfs.
  • The Ndebele followed a patrilineal system (inheritance through the father)
  • Over time, intermarriage between the groups was permitted
  • This helped create a more unified society

6. Sources of Unity in Ndebele Society

  • Common language (IsiNdebele) taught to all assimilated groups
  • Integration into the army to promote loyalty and national identity
  • Royal marriages: The King married women from various clans ‚Äî their role included acting as spies to maintain control
  • Shared loyalty to Mzilikazi and the royal household
  • Uniform military training and age-regiment system

7. Decline of the Ndebele Kingdom

  • Succession crisis after Mzilikazi's death (1868) ‚Äî disputes between his sons, especially over Lobengula's legitimacy
  • European treaties: Lobengula was tricked into signing the Rudd Concession (1888), allowing Cecil Rhodes and the British South Africa Company (BSAC) into Zimbabwe
  • BSAC invasion (1893): The Ndebele were defeated and lost their independence
  • Christian influence: Some indunas who converted to Christianity became disobedient to the king
  • Internal divisions weakened the army and central authority

8. Impact of the Ndebele on Local People

‚úì Positive Effects

  • Introduced centralised political systems
  • Encouraged unity among different tribes
  • Created military discipline and strong defence systems
  • Promoted trade and interaction across regions

‚úó Negative Effects

  • Destroyed villages and displaced local people (especially the Shona and Tswana)
  • Suppressed local cultures ‚Äî many tribes forced to adopt Ndebele language and customs
  • Increased instability through raids
  • Indirectly led to colonisation ‚Äî Lobengula's treaties allowed British occupation, while Lewanika of Barotseland sought British protection from the Ndebele threat
