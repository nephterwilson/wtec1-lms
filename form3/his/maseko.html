The Maseko Ngoni

From Ngwane to Central Malawi

Origin and Leadership

Origin: Ngwane (Maseko clan) defeated by Zwide during the Mfecane period

Leadership Succession

Ngwane Maseko → Magadlela → Mputa → Chidyaonga → Chikuse → Gomani

Migration Route

Escape from Ngwane
Fled to Swaziland after defeat by Zwide's forces
Zimbabwe Entry
Moved into Rozwi land in Zimbabwe
Zambezi Crossing
Crossed the Zambezi River heading north
Mozambique Passage
Passed through Mozambique territory
First Settlement - Domwe Hills
Settled at Domwe Hills in Dedza, Malawi

Why Domwe Hills Was Chosen

  • Good grazing land for their large herds of cattle
  • Local Nyanja were militarily weak and posed little threat
  • Plenty of livestock to raid from surrounding communities
  • Strategic location for defense

Crossing the Shire River

The Great Flight (1837)

  • Fled Domwe in 1837 fearing an attack from the Jere Ngoni
  • Crossed the Shire River with help from Kalonga Sosola
  • Settled in Songea, Tanzania in 1839 with Zulu Gama's group
  • Conflict arose between the groups, and Mputa was killed
  • Chidyaonga became regent after Mputa's death
  • Later moved back to Dedza–Ntcheu (Central Malawi) around 1867

Why Dedza–Ntcheu Was Chosen

  • Plenty of grazing land for their herds
  • Weak Nyanja offered little resistance to settlement
  • Provided protection to local communities against Yao slave raids
  • Fertile land suitable for agriculture

Maseko Ngoni Succession Crisis

The Crisis of 1878

  • Chidyaonga died in 1878
  • His regency ended and Chikuse (son of Mputa) became the rightful king
  • Some supporters backed Chifisi (Chidyaonga's son) instead
  • This dispute led to a major division in the Maseko Ngoni

The Division

Gomani (Chikuse's son) succeeded at Lizulu (Ntcheu)

Kachindamoto (Chifisi's son) ruled at Mtakataka (Dedza)

Civil War

A civil war followed for 3 years between the two factions. Eventually, Gomani defeated Kachindamoto and consolidated power.

Conflict with Colonial Administration (1896)

Gomani I's Resistance

  • Gomani I resisted taxation and forced labour imposed by the British
  • Burned villages near Dombole Mission when missionaries refused to surrender refugees
  • Was arrested by British forces
  • Executed on 27 October 1896
  • After his death, British rule extended fully over the Maseko Ngoni

Socio-Political Organisation of the Ngoni

Political Hierarchy

1. Inkosi ya Makosi (Paramount Chief)
Supreme ruler; head of army and government; above the law
2. Makhosi (District Chiefs)
Ruled large territories under the paramount chief
3. Makosana (Ward Chiefs)
Controlled groups of villages within districts
4. Balumuzana (Nobles)
Clan leaders, mostly from the Jere clan
5. Makhanda (Village Headmen)
Interacted directly with common people
6. Common People
Subjects of the chief; farmers and workers

Inheritance: Patrilineal – power and property passed through the father's line

Indaba: Council of chiefs, indunas, and elders that advised the paramount chief on important matters

Social Structure

Zansi: Original Ngoni people who held positions as rulers and soldiers

Abafo: Assimilated tribes (Nsenga, Tonga, Tumbuka, etc.) who served as workers and farmers

Military Organisation

  • Every male was trained as a soldier from youth
  • Divided by age-regiments called Amabutho:
    • Majaha – young warriors
    • Madoda – adult men forming the main army
  • Each district had its own Impi (army) under a commander
  • Warriors trained in the famous Ngoni fighting techniques

Effects/Impact of the Ngoni Migration

✓ Positive Impacts

  1. Introduced centralised system of government to local societies
  2. Established effective judicial systems with appeals and fairness
  3. Provided protection to weaker tribes against Yao slave traders
  4. Encouraged agriculture and livestock rearing by captives
  5. Promoted unity and resistance against colonial rule (e.g., Gomani I and M'mbelwa)

✗ Negative Impacts

  1. Raids and wars destroyed property and caused loss of lives
  2. Displacement of many tribes from their ancestral land
  3. Cultural suppression – local people forced to adopt Ngoni culture (e.g., Tumbuka became patrilineal)
  4. Loss of population and disruption of traditional kingdoms
