Electromagnetic Waves

Electromagnetic Waves

Electromagnetic waves (EM waves) are waves that can travel through a vacuum. They are generated by vibrating electric charges and consist of both electric and magnetic components.

Electromagnetic Spectrum

The electromagnetic spectrum refers to the entire range of electromagnetic waves that vary in wavelength and frequency. A rainbow shows the visible spectrum of sunlight from red to violet.

Other types include: radio waves, microwaves, infrared, ultraviolet, X-rays, and gamma rays.

Properties of Electromagnetic Waves

  • Speed: Travel at ~3.0×10⁸ m/s (speed of light in vacuum)
  • Wave Equation: c = fλ, where c = speed of light, f = frequency, λ = wavelength
  • Energy: E = hf, h = Planck's constant, f = frequency
  • Transverse Nature: Electric and magnetic fields oscillate perpendicular to the direction of propagation
  • Inverse Square Law: Intensity decreases as I ∝ 1/d²
  • Wave Behavior: Reflection, refraction, diffraction, interference, polarization
  • Straight-Line Travel: Travels in straight lines in a medium with uniform refractive index

Sources and Detection of EM Waves

  • Radio Waves: Transmitters → Detected by TV/radio aerials
  • Microwaves: Ovens → Detected by microwave receivers
  • Infrared (IR): Sun & warm objects → Detected by photodiodes/LDR
  • Visible Light: Sun & lamps → Detected by eyes & photo cells
  • Ultraviolet (UV): Sun & mercury lamps → Detected by photocells/fluorescent chemicals
  • X-rays: X-ray tubes → Detected by films/screens
  • Gamma Rays: Radioactive nuclei → Detected by Geiger-Müller tubes

Frequency and Wavelength

Frequency: Number of waves per second (Hz). Radio waves have the lowest, gamma rays the highest.

Wavelength: Distance covered by one wave cycle (m). Radio waves have the longest, gamma rays the shortest.

Applications of Electromagnetic Waves

  • Radio Waves: Communication, radio, TV, satellites
  • Microwaves: Cooking, communication, air traffic control
  • Infrared: Remote controls, thermography, heating
  • Visible Light: Vision, photosynthesis, lasers, optical fibers
  • Ultraviolet: Fluorescence, sterilization, medicine
  • X-rays: Medical imaging, crystallography
  • Gamma Rays: Cancer treatment, sterilization, flaw detection

Production of Electromagnetic Waves

  • X-rays: Accelerated electrons hitting metal targets
  • Gamma Rays: Radioactive decay in unstable nuclei
  • Ultraviolet: Emitted by very hot objects
  • Visible Light: Detected by human eyes (cones and rods)
  • Radio Waves: Produced by alternating electric currents at specific frequencies
  • Infrared: Emitted by warm objects like human body or fire
