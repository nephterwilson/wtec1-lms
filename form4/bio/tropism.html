What is Plant Response?

Definition

Plant response is how plants react to stimuli from their environment to survive and adapt. Although plants cannot move like animals, they exhibit remarkable responses to environmental changes through growth movements and physiological changes.

‚ö°

Response

The reaction of an organism to a stimulus detected in its environment.

üëÅÔ∏è

Sensitivity (Irritability)

The ability to detect and react to changes; plants have limited sensitivity related mainly to nutrition.

üì°

Types of Stimuli

Light, temperature, humidity, gravity, chemicals, touch, and water.

‚ÜóÔ∏è

Tropisms

Directional growth responses towards or away from stimuli

Example: A plant growing towards a light source (phototropism)

üîÑ

Nasties

Non-directional movement responses not influenced by stimulus direction

Example: Mimosa pudica leaves folding when touched (thigmonasty)

Tropisms: Directional Growth Responses

Tropisms are controlled by plant hormones called auxins

‚òÄÔ∏è

Phototropism

Stimulus: Light

Response: Growth towards light

Example: Shoots and stems bend towards sunlight

Significance: Maximizes photosynthesis for energy production

üåç

Geotropism

Stimulus: Gravity

Response: Roots grow downward (positive), shoots grow upward (negative)

Example: Roots anchor in soil

Significance: Roots absorb water/minerals; shoots reach light

üíß

Hydrotropism

Stimulus: Water

Response: Growth towards water

Example: Roots grow towards moist soil

Significance: Ensures water absorption and mineral uptake

‚öóÔ∏è

Chemotropism

Stimulus: Chemicals

Response: Growth towards chemicals

Example: Pollen tube grows towards ovule

Significance: Essential for fertilization and reproduction

‚úã

Thigmotropism

Stimulus: Touch/Contact

Response: Growth around support

Example: Tendrils coiling around objects

Significance: Provides support for climbing plants

üåª

Heliotropism

Stimulus: Sun movement

Response: Gradual turning towards sun

Example: Sunflowers tracking the sun

Significance: Maximizes light capture (leaf mosaic pattern)

Taxes in Unicellular Organisms

Locomotory responses in single-celled organisms - either positive (towards) or negative (away from) stimulus

Phototaxis

Movement towards light

Example: Chloroplast movement in palisade cells to maximize light absorption

Chemotaxis

Movement towards/away from chemicals

Example: Motile gametes moving towards each other during fertilization

Nasties: Non-Directional Responses

Nasties are rapid, non-directional movement responses that are not influenced by the direction of the stimulus

üåÖ

Photonasty

Light Intensity Response

Stimulus: Changes in light intensity

Response: Rapid opening/closing

Example: Morning glory flowers opening at dawn, closing at dusk

Purpose: Protects reproductive parts and optimizes pollination timing

üåø

Thigmonasty

Touch/Vibration Response

Stimulus: Touch or vibration

Response: Rapid folding or closing

Examples:

  • Mimosa pudica (sensitive plant) - leaves fold when touched
  • Venus flytrap - trap closes on insects

Purpose: Defense mechanism and prey capture (Venus flytrap digests insects)

üß™

Chemonasty

Chemical Response

Stimulus: Chemical substances

Response: Movement of plant parts

Example: Sundew plant tentacles moving to trap insects

Purpose: Carnivorous plants capture and digest prey for nutrients

üí¶

Hydronasty

Water/Humidity Response

Stimulus: Changes in water availability or humidity

Response: Opening or closing movements

Example: Flowers opening/closing with humidity changes

Purpose: Prevents water loss and protects pollen in dry conditions

üçÇ

Abscission

Definition: Shedding of leaves, flowers, or fruits due to adverse conditions

Mechanism: Cells near the leaf base dry out, causing the leaf to fall

Example: Deciduous trees losing leaves in winter to conserve water and energy

üò¥

Dormancy

Definition: Period of very low metabolic activity in seeds and buds

Characteristic: Growth stops temporarily until conditions improve

Purpose: Survival strategy during unfavorable environmental conditions

What are Auxins?

Auxins are plant hormones that control growth responses like tropisms and other plant activities. They are produced mainly at shoot and root tips and move throughout the plant to regulate various processes.

‚Ä¢ Concentration decreases down the stem

‚Ä¢ Cause cell elongation and division

‚Ä¢ Maintain apical dominance (inhibit side branch growth)

‚Ä¢ Prevent early leaf drop (abscission)

‚Ä¢ Maintain leaf attachment to stem

‚Ä¢ Help leaves stay functional longer

‚Ä¢ Regulate leaf development

üåø

Effects on Roots

‚Ä¢ Stimulate root growth at low concentrations

‚Ä¢ Inhibit growth at high concentrations

‚Ä¢ Control root branching

‚Ä¢ Regulate root development

üçé

‚Ä¢ Promote fruit development

‚Ä¢ Prevent premature fruit drop

‚Ä¢ Help fruits reach maturity

‚Ä¢ Control fruit size and shape
