Understanding Electrochemical Cells and Reactions

Basic Concepts of Electrolysis

🔋

Definition of Electrolysis

Electrolysis is the decomposition of a compound in molten or aqueous state using electrical energy. It is carried out in an electrochemical cell.

⚡

Uses Electricity

Electrolysis decomposes molten or aqueous ionic compounds using electricity.

🔄

Ion Movement

Ions must be free to move for electrolysis to occur.

🧪

Compound Types

Works for ionic compounds and covalent compounds that ionize in water.

Components of an Electrochemical Cell

🔋

Dry Cells

Provide direct current for the electrolysis process.

📊

Ammeter/Bulb

Indicates current flow during electrolysis.

🔌

Electrodes

Graphite rods serve as anode (+) and cathode (-).

💧

Electrolyte

Conducts electricity and decomposes during electrolysis.

➕

Anode

Positive electrode connected to positive terminal.

➖

Cathode

Negative electrode connected to negative terminal.

Electrolysis of Molten Ionic Compounds

🔥

Example: Electrolysis of Molten Lead Bromide

Lead ions (Pb²⁺) move to cathode and bromide ions (Br⁻) move to anode, producing lead metal and bromine gas.

➖

At the Cathode (Negative)

Process: Reduction

Pb²⁺ ions gain electrons

Pb²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Pb

Product:

Lead metal is produced

➕

At the Anode (Positive)

Process: Oxidation

Br⁻ ions lose electrons

2Br⁻ → Br₂ + 2e⁻

Product:

Bromine gas is produced

Overall Equation

Pb²⁺ + 2Br⁻ → Pb + Br₂

✓ Metal at Cathode

Lead metal produced at negative electrode

✓ Non-metal at Anode

Bromine gas produced at positive electrode

Writing Half-Equations for Electrode Reactions

Steps:

  • 1. Identify ions and products
  • 2. Positive ions go to cathode
  • 3. Negative ions go to anode
  • 4. Use correct elemental symbols (e.g., Cl₂ not Cl)
  • 5. Balance electrons to match charges

Example: Molten Magnesium Chloride

Cathode:

Mg²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Mg (l)

Anode:

2Cl⁻ → Cl₂ (g) + 2e⁻

Electrolysis of Aqueous Ionic Compounds

💧

Preferential Discharge

In aqueous solutions, water also ionizes, so H⁺ and OH⁻ ions compete with other ions for discharge at electrodes. Discharge depends on reactivity and ion concentration.

➖

At Cathode (Negative)

If metal MORE reactive than hydrogen:

H⁺ ions discharge → Hydrogen gas formed

If metal LESS reactive than hydrogen:

Metal ions discharge → Metal formed

➕

At Anode (Positive)

If halide ions present (Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻):

Halide ions discharge → Halogen gas formed

If NO halide ions present:

OH⁻ ions discharge → Oxygen gas formed

Electrolysis of Brine (Concentrated NaCl Solution)

➖

Cathode

2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂ (g)

Hydrogen gas formed

➕

Anode

2Cl⁻ → Cl₂ (g) + 2e⁻

Chlorine gas formed

💧

Solution

Na⁺ and OH⁻ ions remain

Forms NaOH (Sodium Hydroxide)

Electrolysis of Dilute Sodium Chloride

In dilute solution, different products are formed (essentially electrolysis of water)

Cathode Reaction:

4H⁺ + 4e⁻ → 2H₂ (g)

Hydrogen gas formed

Anode Reaction:

4OH⁻ → O₂ (g) + 2H₂O + 4e⁻

Oxygen gas formed (not chlorine)

Effects of Electrodes on Electrolysis

⚫

Inert Electrodes

(Graphite, Platinum)

Do not react; only allow current flow.

Example: CuSO₄ with inert electrodes

• Cathode: Cu²⁺ → Cu (copper deposits)

• Anode: OH⁻ → O₂ (oxygen gas)

🟤

Copper Electrodes

(Reactive)

Anode participates in reaction.

Example: CuSO₄ with copper electrodes

• Anode: Cu → Cu²⁺ (anode dissolves)

• Cathode: Cu²⁺ → Cu (copper deposits)

Solution color remains unchanged

Uses of Electrolysis

⛏️

Extraction of Metals

Less reactive metals are extracted by dipping a more reactive metal into their aqueous compound, causing displacement.

Example Reaction:

Fe (s) + CuSO₄ (aq) → Cu (s) + FeSO₄ (aq)

Iron displaces copper from copper sulfate solution

✨

Purification of Metals

Impure metal anode dissolves, pure metal deposits at cathode; impurities settle at bottom.

Anode Reaction (Dissolves):

Cu (s) → Cu²⁺ (aq) + 2e⁻

Impure copper dissolves from anode

Cathode Reaction (Deposits):

Cu²⁺ (aq) + 2e⁻ → Cu (s)

Pure copper deposits at cathode

✓ Impurities settle at the bottom as sludge

🎨

Electroplating

Coating a metal with a thin layer of another metal using electrolysis to improve appearance, prevent corrosion, or increase size/strength.

Electroplating Process

1️⃣
Clean

Clean the metal to be plated

2️⃣
Choose

Choose electrolyte with desired metal ions

3️⃣
Connect

Metal to be plated is cathode (-)

4️⃣
Plate

Coating metal is anode (+)

Example: Electroplating Iron with Copper

At Anode (Copper):

Cu → Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻

Copper anode dissolves

At Cathode (Iron):

Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu

Copper deposits on iron

Applications and Benefits of Electroplating

💍

Makes Objects Attractive

Silver-plated jewelry and decorative items have enhanced appearance and value.

🛡️

Protects from Corrosion

Chromium-plated steel bumpers resist rust and weather damage.

💪

Increases Size/Strength

Layering metals can increase thickness and mechanical strength.

⚙️

Industrial Applications

Used for extracting and purifying metals like copper for electrical applications.

Key Takeaways

⚡

Electrolysis Basics

Decomposition of compounds using electrical energy requires free-moving ions in molten or aqueous state.

🔋

Electrode Reactions

Reduction occurs at cathode (gaining electrons), oxidation at anode (losing electrons).

💧

Preferential Discharge

In aqueous solutions, ion reactivity and concentration determine which ions discharge at electrodes.

⛏️

Metal Extraction

Electrolysis is used to extract less reactive metals from their compounds.

✨

Metal Purification

Impure metal dissolves at anode; pure metal deposits at cathode.

🎨

Electroplating

Coating metals improves appearance, prevents corrosion, and increases strength.

⚡

Remember the Flow!

Electrons flow from negative to positive, but positive ions move to cathode and negative ions to anode.
